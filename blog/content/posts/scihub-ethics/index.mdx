The first time I ever laid eyes on a research paper was when I was reading up for my biology project in 12<sup style={{ fontSize: 'small'}}>th</sup> grade. It was about auxin, a phytohormone, but the most memorable part of the paper wasn't its contents, but rather the jaw-dropping 30 USD that Elsevier stated was the price of purchasing the article. To a 17-year-old, 2,247 INR for a single paper seemed beyond absurd. Without a subscription via a library, accessing scholarly articles for the average STEM student is impossible; if I bought 3 papers a day for a month, I would be looking at a bill of 2,00,000 INR. Fortunately for me, this was the time I was introduced to Sci-Hub, the free online repository of academic content that enables readers to access articles normally blocked by paywalls.

It is no secret that publishers charge enormous amounts of money to access their journals, whether you buy a subscription as an institution or purchase individual papers. IISc (Indian Institute of Science) annually spends [11,00,00,000 INR][1] on journal subscriptions, which accounts for nearly 10% of the total institute budget. The question that naturally follows is why is it so expensive? Publishers cite that operational costs are what drive up the price, and it is understandable that a certain amount of money is required for designing, editing, marketing and distribution, but this still does not justify their charges. Neither authors nor peer-reviewers are paid for their work. It is an accepted norm that paying peer-reviewers for their work is unethical, but this calls for its own separate discussion. According to [estimates by JOSS][2] (Journal of Open Source Software), the total cost associated with publishing an article is much less than the standard provided by publishers, which is usually in the ballpark of 1000 USD. After accounting for annual costs such as software infrastructure maintenance, financial services, Crossref, and even providing the editors with a stipend (which few present journals do), the cost per article is around 100 USD. If one were to assume a 30% profit margin, which is the standard in scholarly publishing, this amounts to 130 USD, which is still an order of magnitude less than the [current fees][3] cited by publishers. Editors are invaluable assets to a journal, but they receive no payment for their work. "I've served on editorial boards, advisory boards and countless referee activities and never earned a dime," says Gerard Meijer of the Fritz Haber Institute. It seems to be an accepted phenomenon that such editorial work is unpaid. Recently, a few publishers have begun to pay [stipends][4] to the editors, not as a compensatory move for their work, but rather to prevent the editors from leaving them en masse.

Publishers exploit the fact that research papers are indispensable to academia. Unlike a pair of pants that someone can shop around for in different stores to find the lowest price, a particular paper can only be found in the journal it is published in, and the reader is obligated to pay whatever amount is asked for. One cannot shop around for the paper precisely because the publisher has a monopoly over it due to the nature of copyright laws. When an author submits a manuscript, during the review and publication process, the copyrights are transferred to the journal. Journals say that this transfer enables them to appropriately manage distribution and address any legal concerns as they have in-house legal teams. Demand for research articles is also inelastic, i.e. their demand does not decrease even if the price increases because scientists cannot do without them and libraries end up shelling out money for them.

The biggest opponents of Sci-Hub are the publication giants. Sci-Hub has run into legal trouble several times since its creation. Elsevier filed a [lawsuit][5] against Sci-Hub in 2015 for violating copyright infringement laws, as did the [American Chemical Society][6] in 2017. The complaints filed by other publishers such as Springer Nature, John Wiley and Cambridge University Press have made many internet service providers [block access to Sci-Hub][7]. The reason they feel particularly threatened by Sci-Hub is because it hurts their bottom lines. RELX, Elsevier's parent company, hadÂ revenues of [9.8 billion USD][8] in 2019, at a whopping [31% profit margin][9].

They argue that just as all other businesses in a capitalist economy, they too are entitled to make profits. Unfortunately, in most public research institutions, this profit ultimately comes from taxpayer money; money that could have been spent on upgrading infrastructure, funding important research, or supporting students who cannot afford tuition. It is actually the small, underfunded institutions in developing countries that these high prices hurt most. This high cost also siphons money away from funds used to buy books in libraries. The subscription model is a viable option only for institutes with funding to afford them. It puts research papers out of reach for pretty much everyone else---high school students like I was, independent researchers and the general public. If COVID-19 has shown us anything, it is that people are not familiar enough with basic science. From politicians not appropriately responding to the crisis, to people falling prey to fake news and paying no heed to the precautionary measures stated by public health officials; the pandemic has been exacerbated by a lack of appropriate science communication. Many journals have made papers on coronavirus freely available on the grounds that it is the need of the hour, but isn't all science important in their own contexts? In government funded institutions, the research is paid for by taxpayers, and the results of this research should be accessible to them.

Open access organisations like PLOS vouch for unrestricted access to research papers, and their papers are freely available to all. The catch, however, is that they charge authors a publication fee, which varies from [1500 USD to 3000 USD][10]. This money is either paid by the parent institution or through grants. As we have already seen, this is nearly ten times the estimated cost per article. PLOS has [lost millions of dollars][11], though this has been attributed to poor management of funds. These publication fees are still predatory like journal subscription costs, but instead of the reader carrying the burden, it is the authors. Again, this is money that could have been used more suitably elsewhere.

It is a well-established fact that the current system of academic publication is failing everyone. The reader, the author, the institutions, the taxpayers, the public, the editors; all of them lose out. It is only the publishers who profit, quite literally, from this convoluted system. There are countless academics on the frontlines of the fight to make knowledge freely accessible via truly open source publishing. So where does Sci-Hub fit in? It fits in as an interim solution. While we wait for a complete overhaul of the system, Sci-Hub will provide the open access that is desperately needed. Sci-Hub was born out of the necessity to create a more level playing field. It isn't an entirely perfect solution; where it [sources its papers][12] from is murky and ethically questionable. Sci-Hub uses leaked and stolen user login credentials to gather copyrighted material to add to their repository. They employ phishing to trick unsuspecting individuals into revealing usernames and passwords, which is a breach of privacy.

[Alexandra Elbakyan][13], the founder of Sci-Hub, is often likened to Robin Hood: a person who is technically committing theft, but theft that is aimed at creating a more equal society. Most arguments against it are that it violates copyright laws, but these laws are benefiting publishers and protecting their profits, rather than serving the needs of academia as a whole. So, while the fight for open access continues, Sci-Hub will remain a useful ally. Hopefully, the day will come when we will no longer need it. Until then, to the 17-year-old high school student reading this, Sci-Hub is the desperate measure that was created in desperate times. Just make sure you use a VPN.
